Autism

A set of heterogeneous neurodevelopmental conditions, some early-onset characteristics

  • difficulties in social communication;
  • restricted, repetitive behaviour and interests;
  • worldwide prevalence is 1%;
  • affects more male than famales individuals;
  • comorbidity is common(>70% have concurrent conditions).

Atypical cognitive profiles, which are underpinned by atypical neural development at the systems level

  • impaired social cognition and social perception;
  • executive dysfunction;
  • atypical perceptual and information processing.

Aetiology

genetics + developmentally early environmental factors

Risk

large-effect rare mutation + small-effect comhexmom variants

Intervention

  • early comprehensive and targeted behavioural interventions——improve social communication and reduce anxiety and aggression
  • drugs——reduce comorbid symptoms, but do not directly improve social communication

3. Prognosis and outcome

  • A meta-analysis63 showed that individuals with autism have a mortality risk that is 2·8 times higher (95% CI
    1·8–4·2) than that of unaffected people of the same age and sex. This diff erence is mostly related to co-occurring medical conditions.
  • little is known about how ageing aff ects people with autism

4. Early signs and screening

  • Early identifi cation allows early intervention
  • Variability in age, cognitive ability, and sex leads to differential presentation and the need for appropriate
    screening instruments.
  • Studies of siblings of probands from an early age could potentially identify early behavioural and neural predictors of emerging autism.

5. Clinical ssessment

Diagnostic assessment should be multidisciplinary and use a developmental framework of an interview with the
parent and caregiver, interaction with the individual, collection of information about behaviour in community settings (eg., school reports and job performance), cognitive assessments, and a medical examination. Cooccurring conditions should be carefully screened.

6. Cognition and Neuroscience

  • depict autism —— Kanner’s proposal of an “innate inability to form the usual, biologically provided affective contact with people”

  • the development of cognition and neurobiology is characterised by a complex interplay between innate and environmental factors

  • difficulties in mentalising vs dysfunctions in executive control and language (social vs non-social cognition)

  • self-referential cognition and its neural substrates are also atypical in autism

  • atypical development of the so-called social brain(equal attention should be paid to how these brain structure interact with the rest of the neural system)

  • cognitiove domains in autism resaerch

    1. social cognition and social perception
      • atypical social interaction and social communication
    2. executive function
      • repetitive and stereotyped behaviour
      • atypical social interaction and social communication
    3. bottom-up and top-down (local vs global) information processing
      • idiosyncratic sensory-perceptual processing
      • excellent attention to detail
      • restricted interests and repetitive behaviour
      • atypical social interaction and social communication

7. Neurobiology

  • Neurobiological investigation has identified patterns of brain perfusion and neural biochemical characteristics, which are described elsewhere.
  • Systemslevel connectivity features and plausible neuroanatomical, cellular, and molecular underpinnings of autism have been identified.
  • Autism is characterised by atypical neural connectivity, rather than by a discrete set of atypical brain regions.
  • Although none fully explains all the data, they support the heuristic value of the tenet that neural networks in autism are atypical in various ways.
  • A trajectory of generalised early brain overgrowth when aged 6-24 months (one frequently reported neuroanatomical feature of autism).
  • A reduction in neuron number in the amygdala, fusiform gyrus, and cerebellum, and signs of persistent nrueoimflammation(post-mortem studies).
  • Because of their relation with affiliative and social behaviours, the oxytocin and vasopressin systems’ roles in social impairments in autism are an active focus of investigation, including treatment trials(serotonin and GABA systems).
  • A link between fetal testosterone and autistic traits

8. Genetics

  • Both rare mutations with large effect sizes and commom variations with smaller effect sizes have a role.
  • Some of rare mutations are clinically indentifiable; therefore, screening is recommended as part of routine clinical examination.
  • Contributions from rare and commom genetic variants are not mutually exclusive.
  • The rapid progress of genetics along with animal model systems and systems biology methods will enable the identification of diverse aetiologies and commom molecular and cellular pathways crucial for neurodevelopment for autism.(such clarification could affect how the autisms are classified, diagnosed, and treated in the future)

10. Conclusion

Understanding of autism has changed substantially in the 70 years since it was first described. With the recent
exponential increase in research and the inclusion of scientists from a wide range of disciplines, understanding
will continue to evolve at an accelerated rate. The specialty has achieved much:

  • behavioural definition
  • increased prevalence
  • understanding about early presentation
  • established systemic clinical assessments and evidence-based interventions
  • clarified specific cognitive processes
  • Neurobiology
  • genetic variants, and potential epigenetic and environmental factors
